The incredible story of Britain's most lethal police marksman

FORMER cop firearms officer Tony Long has shot dead three men, and even one of his own bosses joked that he was “the Met’s very own serial killer”.

But the most lethal police marksman in the UK has never lost a wink of sleep over the lives he has taken.

In fact, the only thing that sometimes bothers him is why he does not feel bothered.

In a new book about his life in the police team criminals crudely dubbed “the Execution Squad”, the retired dad-of-two writes: “Over the years, alone with my thoughts, I had contemplated many times how it was I’d coped with that responsibility without feeling guilt or remorse.

“Sometimes I’d even wonder if there was something wrong with me, but I’d always come to the same conclusion.

“They had all been criminals prepared to use knives and guns to intimidate, wound or even kill people weaker or more vulnerable than themselves and I had simply acted to stop them.”

The first time Tony, now 59, killed was in 1987, when he and fellow officers swooped on a raid by three armed robbers on a Securicor van delivering wages in South London.

He shot dead 24-year-olds Michael Flynn and Nicholas Payne, fearing they were about to shoot the security van’s driver. A third armed robber was also shot but survived.

Describing how he took out Flynn he says: “I remembered seeing his eyes through the slot of his balaclava as I fired.

“The other two were also starting to turn and bring their weapons to bear so I’d had to start shooting or it might have been me waiting on a slab for the pathologist’s knife.

“My rounds had hit him (Flynn) in the back, tough. This was big boys’ rules, he should’ve stuck to shoplifting.” An inquest found the two crooks had been killed lawfully.

The next time Tony killed, in April 2005, it took far longer to prove he had made the right decision.

He spent ten years clearing his name after shooting dead suspected gangster Azelle Rodney, 24, in April 2005.

The officer was part of the Met’s CO19 firearms unit called in after a tip-off that a British gang intended to rob Colombian drug dealers. Tony’s team was told the Brits were armed with MAC-10 sub-machine guns.

The cops soon found themselves in a car chasing Rodney’s VW Golf along a road in Mill Hill, North London.

Tony swung into action when he thought he saw Rodney reach for a weapon.

He writes: “He was armed, I was certain of it! He’d picked up the heavy, box-like MAC-10 and was readying himself to fire.

“I held my breath as I aligned my iron sights on his centre mass, my finger curled around the trigger.

“No more time. I was going to have to shoot him and I was going to have to do it now. At any second my colleagues would be on foot and vulnerable. No time to shout a warning!”

Tony fired eight times through the Golf’s open window at close range, killing Rodney instantly.

As ambulance crews attempted in vain to save the suspect, Tony writes that he looked on, thinking: “A life wasted through reckless, selfish, greed. I was starting to feel angry.

“Angry at a nameless corpse going cold in a suburban London street. The body of a young, stupid gangster who had forced me into killing, again.”

A search of the suspect’s car then revealed three firearms inside but none was an automatic and only one was loaded.

And few days later Long admitted that, on reflection, he was just assuming that Rodney had picked up the weapon, rather than actually seeing it.

In a full written statement at the time he explained: “Everything about his actions and his body language led me to believe that he had picked up a firearm and was preparing to shoot a fully automatic firearm but I still couldn’t see a weapon.”

A lengthy public inquiry, chaired by retired High Court judge Sir Christopher Holland, eventually ruled that Tony had acted unlawfully. As a result, Tony was charged with murder and stood trial.

He was finally found not guilty by a jury last year.

Despite the saga, and expressing sympathy for the dead man’s family, Tony writes: “I’d not lost a moment’s sleep over Rodney’s death.

“Rodney wasn’t Stephen Lawrence, he was a violent gangster already wanted for a double stabbing.”

But he has sometimes wished he had never come clean in his statement about not having actually seen Rodney with a gun.

He writes: “In in my darker moments, I have laid awake wondering whether it might not have been a lot simpler to have simply said that I’d seen that gun in Rodney’s hand.

“Thinking of what my family and I had been put through, I sometimes thought that perhaps, honesty wasn’t always the best policy.”

Tony, who grew up in Sussex, joined the Metropolitan Police in 1975. He later did a five-day firearms training course, and after scoring 100 per cent in the accuracy test, he joined the Met’s firearms unit in 1983.

Two years later, on Christmas Day 1985, he was involved in his first shooting. Errol Walker had fatally stabbed his ex-wife’s step-sister in her flat in Northolt, West London, then barricaded himself inside holding her daughter Karlene, four, at knifepoint.

Walker, 30, cut the little girl’s arm to the bone and let her blood drip out the window on firefighters waiting below in case he dropped her.

Eventually the order was given for Tony and his team to storm the flat.
He writes: “As I started to fire he plunged the knife into the little girl.

“I fired a double tap, my muzzle blast lighting the room and silhouetting my sights. I knew from his reaction that I’d hit him.”

Walker survived and was sentenced to life for murder. Tony was commended for bravery — one of seven such commendations he picked up in his often illustrious 33-year career.

He was part of the squad that dressed up as cleaners to foil a daring heist of De Beers diamonds worth £350million on show at London’s Millennium Dome in 2000. It led to this Sun headline.

But he admits he never felt secure enough in his job not to have back-up plans in place.

He reveals how he and a colleague collected a “Marmite file” — named after a TV advert at the time that said “my mate Marmite” — of incriminating information on supervisors.

Tony says: “The idea was that if you found yourself in a corner with no way out, you’d pull out the file and before revealing what you had on them, would say, ‘You don’t want to sack me, sir. I’m your mate’.

“Our file was bulging with misdemeanours and f***-ups. Some might consider it blackmail, we preferred to call it insurance.”

Tony was also awarded £5,000 compensation after Sue Akers, then Met’s Internal Affairs Commander, jokingly called him “the Met’s very own serial killer” in 2005 after the Rodney case.

The incident highlighted conflicting attitudes towards armed police officers even within the Met.

Tony writes: “In a police service proud to boast that it is predominantly unarmed, those that volunteer to carry firearms have always been looked upon as its b*****d sons and daughters.

“Still the stigma remains.”
